Roles of cell cycle kinase CDKs in mitochondrial homeostasis in cellular radiation response

2013 - present
UC Davis Department of Radiation Oncology, UC Davis Cancer Center, Sacramento, CA

Mammalian cells are able to induce an adaptive response to enhance their survival under certain genotoxic stress conditions including ionizing radiation. However, the exact mechanism causing such adaptive radiation response is unknown. Mitochondrial homeostasis is proven actively involved in radiation induced cellular adaptive response. Elucidate the mechanisms underlying radiation-induced mitochondrial homeostasis will help to develop efficient strategy to improve the outcome of tumor radiotherapy and protect normal cells from radiation-associated injury.

Radiation induces DNA damage and repair of damaged DNA is an energy consuming process; however, it is unclear how DNA repair is fueled and whether mitochondrial energy production contributes to nuclear DNA repair. We found that sublethal dose (5 Gy) of radiation enhances mitochondrial ATP generation, which is paralleled with enhanced mitochondrial relocation of cell cycle kinase CDK1 and nuclear DNA repair capacity. More interestingly, mitochondria targeting expression of kinase deficient mutant CDK1 significantly reverses radiation-enhanced mitochondrial ATP generation and inhibits DNA repair; and overexpression of CDK1 phosphorylation deficient mitochondrial respiratory chain complex I (CI) subunits compromises radiation-enhanced mitochondria ATP generation. These results suggest a unique mechanism governing the communication between mitochondria and nucleus, in which mitochondria-relocated CDK1 boosts cellular bioenergetics to meet the increased cellular fuel demand for DNA repair and cell survival under genotoxic stress.

Recently, we reported that a small fraction of Cyclin B1/CDK1 is localized in the mitochondrial matrix, which is further enriched at late G2 phase and phosphorylates a cluster of CI subunits. CDK1-mediated CI subunit phosphorylation boosts mitochondrial ATP generation, allowing cells to sense and respond to increased energy demand for G2/M transition for successful cell-cycle progression (2, 7). Interestingly, Cyclin D1/CDK4 is also found relocated in mitochondria under low dose ionizing radiation, where it phosphorylates MnSOD, causing enhanced MnSOD activity and mitochondrial respiration, allowing cells to sense environmental genotoxic stress to improve cell survival (5). Meanwhile, we found that sublethal dose (5 Gy) of radiation induces a feture of energy shift from glycolysis to mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation in multiple types of tumor cell including breast cancer, colon cancer and glioma brain tumor cells (6), and that CDK1-mediated posttranslational modulation (phosphorylation) of mitochondrial proteins (e.g. SIRT3) results in enhanced mitochondrial bioenergetics and mitochondria homeostasis, which may contribute to the adaptive radioresistance in colon cancer cells (3). Accumulating evidence indicated that cancer stem cells contribute to tumor radioresistance, but the mechanisms is not clear. We have rescently isolated fractionated ionizing radiation (FIR)-surviving clones from multiple types of tumor cells and found that this FIR-surviving population represent cancer stem cell and aggressiveness features and alteration of mitochondrial energy metabolism (data not published), suggesting that these small population of stem-like FIR-surviving cells may contribute to cancer radioresistance and relapse. Elucidation of the mechanisms underlying cancer stem cell radioresistance will provide new targets to improve the outcome of cancer therapy.

Identification of cis-regulatory elements of nautilus gene in Drosophila

2010 - 2012
LBMB, National Cancer Institute, National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD

A cis-regulatory element is a non-coding DNA sequence in or near a gene required for proper spatiotemporal expression of that gene, often containing binding sites for transcription factors. We identified cis-regulatory elements of nautilus gene in Drosophila as a start to study how this gene's expression is regulated.

We predicted some conserved sequences in 8kb upstream region and introns of nautilus gene as its tentative cis-regulatory elements with a bioinformatics tool, EvoPrinter, which employs a modified BLAT algorithm (enhanced BLAT) to compare alignments between evolutionarily distant genomes (12 Drosophila genomes in this case). Then Gal4-UAS reporter system and RNA in situ hybridization in Drosophila embryos were done to verify those sequences. Six sequences were proven involved in the regulation of nautilus gene expression in different spacial and temporal manner.

Transgenic fly line for functional study of nautilus gene in Drosophila muscle development

2009 - 2011
LBMB, National Cancer Institute, National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD

An schematic overview of SIRT
Gao, PNAS, 105(37), 13999 –14004, 2008

Gene targeting is a genetic technique that uses homologous recombination to change an endogenous gene.Ends-in makes use of p-element transposon insertion, FLP-FRT site-specific recombination system, I-SceI rare-cutting endonuclease mediated DSB and homologous recombination (HR) repair system to target desired mutation into the interest locus in the genome (Rong, Y. Science, 288, 2013-2018, 2000). PhiC31 integrase mediates sequence-directed, irreversible and highly efficient integration between a bacterial attachment site (attB) and a phage attachment site (attP). SIRT combines phiC31-mediated site-specific integration and ends-in targeting to generate mutations of a gene at its endogenous locus. An attP site is firstly targeted to the interest locus through ends-in targeting and subsequent reduction. Further modifications of the same locus are introduced by integration of an attB-containing plasmid with a modified target locus. Thereafter, a series of modification of the same target locus can be introduced repeatedly with once attP placement step. It is a laber- and time-saving tool that allows repeated modifications of a gene, including single amino acid replacements, small domain deletion and complete deletion (Gao, G. PNAS, 105(37), 13999 –14004, 2008).

Using SIRT, we established a transgenic fly line with an attP site inserted into nautilus gene in Drosophila genome, which can be used to study the function of nautilus gene by repeatedly introducing a series of modifications. It has been used in a study for deletion of entire 3’UTR region and mutations of some candidate miRNA binding sites to verify the targeting effect of series of miRNAs on nautilus gene. In addition, based on this fly line, 3’ end tagged fly line can be easily established and used in ChIP with the antibody to the tag to screen the candidate targets of nautilus.

Aurora-A interacts with Cyclin B1 and play roles in tumorigenesis

2006 - 2008
Cancer Institute, Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences, Beijing, China

Aurora-A, as a cell cycle regulator, has been reported overexpressed in many types of human cancers, leading to centrosome amplification and aneuploidy, which can be a driving cause of genomic instability and tumorigenesis. We found that overexpression of Aurora-A delays Cyclin B1 degradation, resulting in an increased Cyclin B1 protein level, while Aurora-A RNAi enhances Cyclin B1 degradation. Further study showed that Aurora-A interacts with Cyclin B1 and inhibites ubiquitin-mediated degradation of Cyclin B1, which depends on Aurora-A kinase activity. These results provide new insight into the mechanism of how deregulated Aurora-A contributes to genomic instability and carcinogenesis

Contributions:

  • Establishment of Aurora-A overexpression and knockdown ESCC cell model
  • Immunohistonchemistry and immunofluroscence assay
  • Preparation of total RNA from cells and performance of RT-PCR
  • Preparation of protein sample and performance of immunoblot
  • Immunoprecipitation and pull-down assay to analyze protein interaction
  • DNA cloning and cell transfection

Production of polyclonal and monoclonal antibody against cardiac troponin-I (cTn-I)

2003 - 2005
Institute of Radiation Medicine, Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences, Tianjin, China

Antibodies are host proteins that are produced by the immune system in response to foreign molecules that enter the body. These foreign molecules are called antigens, and their molecular recognition by the immune system results in selective production of antibodies that are able to bind the specific antigen. Antibodies are made by B-lymphocytes and circulate throughout the blood and lymph where they bind to their specific antigen, enabling it to be cleared from circulation. This ability of animal immune systems to produce antibodies capable of binding specifically to antigens can be harnessed to manufacture probes for detection of molecules of interest in a variety of research and diagnostic applications.

Antibody production involves preparation of antigen samples and their safe injection into laboratory or farm animals so as to evoke high expression levels of antigen-specific antibodies in the serum, which can then be recovered from the animal. Polyclonal antibodies are recovered directly from serum (bleeds). Monoclonal antibodies are produced by fusing antibody-secreting spleen cells from immunized mice with immortal myeloma cell to create monoclonal hybridoma cell lines that express the specific antibody and secrete into cell culture supernatant.

Using cTn-I protein isolated from patient heart or commercially synthesized cTn-I polypeptied, we produced the high titer rabbit polyclonal antibody and mouse monoclonal antibody against human cardiac troponin-I (cTn-I), which can be used in lab research and clinical diagnosis.

Contributions:

  • Production of rabbit polyclonal antibody against cTn-I: Rabbit immunolization though i.p. injection, serum collection, and test of antibody titer using enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA).
  • Production of mouse monoclonal antibody against cTn-I: mouse immunolization though i.p. injection, isolation of spleen cells, creation of monoclonal hybridoma cell lines, collection of cell culture supernatant containing the specific antibody, and test of antibody titer using ELISA.
